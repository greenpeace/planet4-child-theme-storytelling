Storytelling is not new to Greenpeace. The story Greenpeace tells, and has always told, is that a better world is possible, and brave individual and collective actions can make it a reality. Every Greenpeace campaign and every Greenpeace action can be boiled down to this idea, the moral of our organizational story:

A billion acts of courage can spark a better tomorrow.

But no matter how compelling a story we tell externally, it will be hollow and ultimately unconvincing unless we live its values. For us to succeed in creating a collective story that promotes hope, courage and a recognition of our interconnectivity with each other and the planet we call home, we must embody it, and invite others to co-create our story with us. We must walk the talk.

For at least the past seven years, many story lovers at Greenpeace have contributed their vision for how we might do this: what might story work at Greenpeace look like and how might Greenpeace put courage, connection and hope at the core of our work? T They helped to birth the Seven Shifts, some directions rather than destinations for how we, as Greenpeace, might live the moral of our organisational story. Different offices and different projects will be at different stages of living these shifts. They are intended to be aids to decision making and evaluation. Does this new project or piece of work further one or more of these transitions? If not, how can we make it do so? We are on a journey together to be the organisation that the world needs, and to drive the world towards the more beautiful future that we know to be possible. These Seven Shifts aim to give us a common map to steer by.
